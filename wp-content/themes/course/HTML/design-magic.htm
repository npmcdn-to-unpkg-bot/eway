When envisioning the new Harry Potter stamps, the Postal Service and Warner Bros. worked some wizardry of their own.
  • Story by Kristen Kimmel
  • Stamp art direction by Bill Gicker & Greg Breeding
  • Stamp design by Greg Breeding

A boy receives an unexpected letter, marked with an unusual wax seal and addressed to him at “The Cupboard under the Stairs.” A flurry of letters follows, inviting him into a world of wizardry, adventure, and magic.

While these events are fictional — first played out in J.K. Rowling’s novels, then cast onto the big screen by Warner Bros. — their enchanting effect is real. Since the first Harry Potter book hit stores in the U.K. in 1997, more than 450 million copies in 74 different languages have been sold. The movie series by Warner Bros., a 10-year saga that began in 2001, is the highest grossing franchise of all time.

Now, the tale is appearing through yet another medium.

“Harry Potter has had such an impact on American culture, and these stamps celebrate that,” says USPS creative director Bill Gicker. “The books sparked kids’ interest in reading again, and the films are such a spectacular rendition of the stories.”

Stamp designer Greg Breeding is no stranger to Harry Potter’s epic world. His children grew up reading the books and watching the movies, and he joined in along with them. Yet his familiarity with the subject didn’t spare him a classic design challenge: finding creative solutions within the format.

The first constraint was self-imposed. “I made the decision pretty early on that the stamps should be character-based,” Breeding says. “There are a lot of wonderful aspects of Harry Potter like the Hogwarts Express, Hogwarts castle, and Hogsmeade, but the scale and extensive detail of those scenes wouldn’t work well at stamp size.”

About the stamp booklet

The first block introduces main characters as we meet them in the first movie, Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone: Harry Potter as he curiously examines a letter from Hogwarts; and Ron Weasley and Hermione Granger, who quickly become Harry’s best friends and allies in his fight against the Dark Forces of the wizarding world.

The second block features some of the most beloved creatures from the films (Fawkes, Hedwig, Buckbeak, and Dobby), while the third block pays tribute to the important Hogwarts professors: Albus Dumbledore, Severus Snape, Minerva McGonagall, and Rubeus Hagrid.

The fourth block of stamps revisits major characters at a later point in the story, including an older Hermione, Harry, and Ron; eccentric but loyal Luna Lovegood; confident and courageous Ginny Weasley; and twins and pranksters Fred and George Weasley. The fifth and final block shows notorious villains — Draco Malfoy, Bellatrix Lestrange, and Voldemort — as well as Harry Potter in the last battle against the Dark Lord.

With 20 stamps to work with, Breeding knew he couldn’t feature all of Harry Potter’s colorful characters. Together with Warner Bros., he narrowed down a decade’s worth of characters to a core group: personalities like Harry, Ron, and Hermione; the notable professors; even a few villains. Since the United States Postal Service doesn’t feature living people on stamps, Breeding had to be careful to show the actors as characters in costume, portrayed within the context of the movie.

Then, Warner Bros. gave Breeding access to a collection of preapproved movie stills, which served as the basis for the stamp designs. While the collection was large — about 10,000 deep — the images themselves presented an inherent challenge.

“Film and stamp composition are drastically different,” Breeding explains. “These movie stills are designed to be presented at 70 feet wide.” Stamps, of course, are about one inch wide.

Click and drag to see how a movie still was cropped to fit the stamp format.

Breeding combed the collection over and over, searching for stills that could be cropped to fit the format. He handpicked images with bright colors, as some of the movies reflect a darker aesthetic that would look subdued at a smaller scale.

With images chosen, Breeding began to place them as a pane of 20 stamps — a format he quickly abandoned. The stamps show a variety of characters from different films in the series, so displaying them all together resulted in a busy, disjointed design.

Pushing creative limits yet again, Breeding decided to pursue an entirely new stamp format — a five-panel booklet that invites exploration, much like a reader paging through chapters of a book or a viewer returning to favorite scenes in the films. Each panel highlights a special part of the story: the main characters in the first movie; the beloved creatures; the important professors; the characters at an older age; and the notorious villains and final battle.

To unify the designs, he placed a Hogwarts seal at the center of each block of stamps — a small reminder of the enchanting world. The back of the booklet sets the tone for the entire concept: One panel includes a summary of the series and another shows an illustration of Hogwarts. A picture of Harry playing Quidditch on the cover balances the adventure with lightheartedness, just like the books and movies.

Though this small stamp booklet folds to a mere four-by-four inches, it successfully summarizes an intricate story — one that is still being told, retold, and enjoyed.

And just like it did with Harry, now a simple letter marked with a stamp can take us all into that mysterious world, too.
